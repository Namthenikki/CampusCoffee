# Campus Coffee — why these mechanics work

The behavioral design rationale behind every mechanic. Grounded in established research: Nir Eyal's Hook model, B.J. Fogg's behavior model (B = MAP), variable-ratio reinforcement (Skinner), the Zeigarnik effect, the IKEA/investment effect, loss aversion (Kahneman & Tversky), and social proof (Cialdini). The goal is Instagram-grade pull with none of Instagram's guilt: every loop below terminates in a *real-world meeting*, not more screen time.

## The core stance: scarcity beats the feed

Tinder's swipe is a variable-ratio slot machine on an **infinite reel** — engagement without consequence, which is why it produces burnout and ghosting. Campus Coffee inverts it:

- **Three pours a day.** The daily batch is small, curated, and gone when it's gone. Scarcity raises the perceived value of each token (commodity theory) and makes checking the app a *ritual* (fixed-interval anticipation, like BeReal's daily moment) rather than a compulsion.
- **A token, not a card stack.** Tokens are things you *redeem*. Every visual in the app — perforated edge, serial number, rubber stamp — says "this is a ticket to something real," priming commitment instead of evaluation.
- **No infinite anything.** When the pot's empty, the app says so and sends you back to your life. Tomorrow's pour is the retention hook — not one more scroll.

## Loop by loop

**Trigger → Action → Variable reward → Investment** (the Hook), mapped:

| Stage | Mechanic |
|---|---|
| External trigger | Daily pour lands; blind-window countdown; "decide" stamp on Today |
| Internal trigger | 8 pm mess loneliness; exam-week panic; "who is this person?" curiosity |
| Action (made tiny — Fogg) | One tap: Pair up / Lock slot / Send a brew |
| Variable reward | Who's in today's pour (variable), whether a brew comes back (variable), who the blind match *is* (the biggest variable of all) |
| Investment | Profile, timetable, streaks, learned weights — all make *your* copy of the app smarter and harder to abandon |

**The 48-hour blind window** is the flagship psychological device:

- A **deadline** converts chat from open-ended (dies of entropy, like most Tinder matches) into a shared project with a climax. Loss aversion does the heavy lifting — the window closing *costs* you something.
- **Anonymity removes the photo-judgment layer** that makes dating apps shallow and appearance-anxious. Conversation quality becomes the only signal — which selects for exactly the users you want.
- The **Zeigarnik effect** (open loops demand closure) keeps the match on the mind all 48 hours. The countdown in the header makes the open loop visible.
- The **private mutual-yes gate** removes rejection cost. Nobody ever learns they were declined — the failure mode is silence, not humiliation. Low downside → more honest yeses.

**Streaks (mess pairs)**: endowed progress + loss aversion, borrowed from Snapchat — but the streak counts *shared meals eaten*, so maintaining it means eating dinner with a friend, not opening an app. Proven pairs auto-resurface ("Again?") because habit formation needs the same cue → same routine.

**The reveal selfie**: peak-end rule. The date ends with a cooperative, slightly giddy act (one shared photo revealing both faces at once) — the memory of the whole experience is colored by its ending. "Not a match" gets a dignified ending too: solo pics, no forced cooperation, no shame.

**Social proof, honest**: "12 study sessions locked this week" — real counts from the real campus, never fabricated. At a small college, *specific* small numbers are more credible and more contagious than big fake ones.

**Timetable investment**: the library feature's match quality is capped by timetable honesty, so the grid is one-tap-per-cell and instantly rewarded (candidates appear as overlap appears). Effort → ownership (IKEA effect) → retention.

## The lines we don't cross

Addictive-like-Instagram is the pull; these are the brakes, and they're features, not compliance:

- **Reliability score is invisible.** Public shame scores (or visible "ratings") poison campus social dynamics. It only quietly reorders a queue.
- **No read receipts, no "online now", no typing indicators in blind phase.** Anxiety mechanics create engagement and misery in equal measure.
- **Honest cancellation is free.** Punishing people for communicating creates no-shows; we only punish silence.
- **Every loop exits to real life.** The success metric is checkins (two phones at one cafe for 10 minutes), not session length. If session length grows while checkins don't, the product is failing.
- **Blur is unilateral and reversible.** Consent to be seen is per-person, never negotiable by the other side, never expired by time.

## Aesthetic rationale ("no chapri" clause)

Dark-roast palette because campus phone life happens 10 pm–2 am and premium apps respect the hour. One accent color for action (burnt honey), one reserved exclusively for the dating layer (chai-spice red) — color *means* something. Bricolage Grotesque display + Space Mono serials give it the feel of a printed canteen token, not a startup template. Hinglish in flavor copy ("Shaam ki chai"), plain English on every functional control — code-switching like the users do, never where it costs clarity.
